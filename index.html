The Province

Where History Was Written in Stone

Paktia is one of the 34 provinces of Afghanistan, nestled in the rugged southeastern reaches of the country. Covering approximately 6,400 square kilometers, it sits at the edge of the Hindu Kush mountain range — a land of deep valleys, high passes, and forested highlands that have shaped both its people and its destiny.

Its capital, Gardez, has served for centuries as a cultural and commercial hub for the region. The province borders Pakistan to the east, making it a strategically vital corridor that has drawn the attention of empires, conquerors, and powers across millennia.

Part of the larger historical region known as Loya Paktia — or "Greater Paktia" — the province shares deep cultural and ethnic ties with neighboring Khost and Paktika. Together, these provinces form one of the most distinctly Pashtun heartlands in all of Afghanistan.

Years of History
A Journey Through Time

The History of Paktia

Paktia has been a crossroads of civilizations for millennia. Its mountain corridors have witnessed the passage of armies, traders, prophets, and conquerors — each leaving a mark on the land and its people.

Ancient Era

The Achaemenid Empire

Among the earliest major powers to exert influence over Paktia was the Achaemenid Persian Empire. The region formed part of the eastern frontiers of this vast empire, serving as a strategic highland gateway between the Iranian plateau and the Indian subcontinent. The Pashtun ancestors of the region's people have inhabited these mountains for thousands of years.

4th Century BC

Alexander the Great

The armies of Alexander of Macedon swept through Afghanistan on their march east, passing through or near the territories that would become Paktia. The region's rugged terrain and fierce local resistance offered a preview of what many future conquerors would face — that these mountains do not yield easily.

7th–8th Century

The Coming of Islam

With the rise of the Arab Caliphates and the spread of Islam across Central Asia, the people of Paktia gradually adopted the faith that would become the defining pillar of their identity and culture. Islam wove itself into the tribal codes and customs of the Pashtun people, creating a synthesis of Islamic law and ancient Pashtunwali tradition.

18th Century

The Durrani Empire

Under Ahmad Shah Durrani, the founder of modern Afghanistan, Paktia became part of the powerful Durrani Empire. This era cemented Pashtun political dominance in the region and gave Paktia's tribes a central role in the empire's military and governance structures. The province's warriors gained a reputation as formidable fighters that endures to this day.

19th Century

The Anglo-Afghan Wars

During the three Anglo-Afghan Wars, Paktia's eastern border position made it a flashpoint of colonial-era conflict. British imperial forces repeatedly clashed with Pashtun tribes defending their highland territories. The province became synonymous with resistance — a reputation deeply embedded in local identity and oral tradition.

1979–1989

The Soviet-Afghan War

Perhaps no era shaped modern Paktia more profoundly than the Soviet invasion. The province became a critical front in the decade-long conflict. Mujahideen fighters used its mountain passes as infiltration routes and supply corridors. The Battle of Ali Khel and operations around Gardez drew international attention. Paktia produced some of the war's most notable commanders and resistance leaders.

1990s

Civil War

The collapse of the Soviet-backed government plunged Afghanistan into brutal civil war. Paktia's tribal factions participated in the shifting alliances and conflicts of this era, suffering significant displacement and destruction. The powerful Zadran tribe and other Pashtun confederations navigated this period with the survival instincts honed over centuries of conflict.

2001–2021

Post-9/11 Era

Following the US-led invasion of Afghanistan after September 11, 2001, Paktia became a major theater of operations due to its border location and tribal dynamics. The province saw significant coalition military activity, development projects, and ongoing insurgency. Despite the challenges, Paktia also experienced improvements in education, health infrastructure, and connectivity during this period.

2021 – Present

Taliban Control

In August 2021, as US and coalition forces withdrew, the Taliban rapidly gained control of Paktia province. The transition marked another chapter in the province's long history of shifting powers. The resilient people of Paktia — farmers, traders, and tribal elders — continue to navigate life under the new administration as they have adapted to every change before it.

People of the Land

The Tribes of Paktia

Paktia is predominantly Pashtun, with a small Tajik minority. The province is governed not just by formal administration but by the powerful tribal structures that have defined life here for generations. These confederations form the social fabric of every village and valley.

Zadran

The most prominent tribal confederation in Paktia, the Zadran have long shaped the province's political and military landscape. Known for fierce independence and a strong warrior tradition.

Mangal

One of the key Pashtun tribes of the region, the Mangal inhabit significant parts of Paktia and are known for their fierce attachment to tribal autonomy and Pashtunwali code.

Jaji

The Jaji tribe occupies the Jaji district of Paktia and played a prominent role in the anti-Soviet resistance during the 1980s, gaining international recognition for their fierce resistance.

Chamkani

Inhabiting the northeastern districts, the Chamkani are deeply rooted in the agricultural traditions and pastoral life of the highlands, maintaining strong tribal customs and loyalty.

Ahmadzai

A branch of the Ghilzai Pashtuns, the Ahmadzai have connections across southeastern Afghanistan and have produced notable political and military figures throughout Afghan history.

Tajik Minority

A small but historically present Tajik community adds to Paktia's ethnic tapestry, predominantly in the capital Gardez, contributing to trade and the urban economy.

Traditions & Way of Life

Culture of Paktia

The culture of Paktia is inseparable from Pashtunwali — the ancient, unwritten code of the Pashtuns. This code governs everything from hospitality and honor to justice and revenge. It predates Islam in the region and yet has absorbed and coexisted with Islamic values for over a thousand years.

Melmastia (hospitality) is perhaps the most celebrated principle — a guest in Paktia is treated with extraordinary generosity regardless of the host's own means. Strangers become family at the threshold of a Paktia home.

The province's cuisine reflects its agricultural bounty: lamb kebabs, Kabuli pulao fragrant with raisins and spices, hearty stews, and flatbreads baked in clay ovens. Pomegranates and apricots grow in the valleys, while wheat and maize cover the arable land.

Traditional Pashtun music, the rubab lute, and the dhol drum form the soundtrack of celebrations and gatherings. The Attan — a circular folk dance — brings communities together at weddings and festivals in joyful, rhythmic motion.

Notable sites include the Khair Kot Fort, an ancient fortress showcasing Afghanistan's architectural heritage; the Shah-i-Khuda Mosque in Gardez with its intricate tilework; and the breathtaking Shwak Valley, a lush highland landscape beloved by those who have walked its trails.
